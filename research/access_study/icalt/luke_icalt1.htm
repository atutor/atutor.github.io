Objectives:

  • investigate barriers faced by individuals in Web based learning environments
  • educate the general public and governing bodies about accessibility issues associated with Web based instruction
  • make recommendations to courseware developers for methods to increase their products’ support of accessible design
  • provide guidelines for course designers and instructors to ensure the accessibility of their curriculum materials.

Study focus groups:

  • Blind
  • Vision Impaired
  • Mobility Impaired
  • Learning Disabled
  • Experienced and inexperienced users

Courseware tested:

  • BlackBoard CourseInfo 4.0
  • Web Course in a Box
  • Mallard 2000b
  • WebCT 2.1
  • Virtual-U 2.5
  • TopClass 3.1.2

Courseware environments need to consider problems in the design of the technology itself, as well as with the pedagogy directing its use.

Accessibility Issues by Disability Group

Blind

  • Java: chats, whiteboards, progress displays
  • Browse buttons
  • Poorly labeled form elements
  • Inconsistencies in layout
  • Inconsistencies in language
  • Multiple frames and nested tables
  • Surprise popup windows

Accessibility Issues by Disability Group

Low Vision

  • Like those experienced by blind students
  • Can fall back on residual vision, so problems somewhat ameliorated
  • Problems exacerbated by limited experience with web navigation and courseware environments in general, inexperience with the adaptive technology, and general assumptions and preconceptions of online learning. 
  • Other problems included the illogical display of steps required for task completion, and confusing and ambiguous use of terminology

Accessibility Issues by Disability Group

Learning Disability

  • Inconsistencies in layout
  • Inconsistencies in language
  • Absence of alternative formats
  • Multi-step activities were confusing due to lack of instructions

Mobility Impaired

  • No difficulties

Conclusions: Recommendations for Inclusive Design

Problems can be compounded by the following:

  • Internet experience (or lack thereof)
  • Assistive technology experience (or lack thereof)
  • Complexities in page layouts and lack of adequate instructions
  • OS/default settings (eg. mail program)

“For people without disabilities, technology makes things convenient; for people with disabilities, it makes things possible.”
